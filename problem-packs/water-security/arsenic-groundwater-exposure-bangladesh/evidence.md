## Evidence Notes

### WHO Arsenic Fact Sheet

Use this source for the health-based guideline value (10 micro-grams per litre), the classification of arsenic as a major public health chemical, and the health-effects framing. Do not use it as proof of Bangladesh-specific exposure levels.

### BGS/DPHE Phase 1 Survey (2001)

Use this source for the foundational national survey of arsenic in Bangladesh tube wells. It establishes that contamination is widespread (46 percent above WHO guideline), varies by district and depth, and provides the baseline for any subsequent testing-coverage analysis. Limitation: 1998-1999 data, district-level grain only.

### EHP Systematic Review (2024)

Use this source for the population-exposure estimate (35-77 million) and the cancer-risk dose-response relationship. Treat the range as reflecting genuine uncertainty, not a point estimate. Do not use the exposure estimate as a current testing-coverage figure.

### BAMWSP Implementation Report (2006)

Use this source for the scale of the initial testing campaign (1.4 million wells by 2006) and the documented unevenness of coverage. Limitation: the figure is 20 years old and does not account for wells installed after 2006. The well-marking campaign's behavioural impact is not fully documented.

### Flanagan et al. (2012)

Use this source for the well-depth-arsenic relationship, which is critical for prioritising which untested wells to test first. The non-monotonic depth-concentration relationship means depth alone cannot predict safety without testing.

### WorldPop Bangladesh Population Data

Use this source for population denominators at upazila and union grain. Do not use population proximity as a direct proxy for arsenic exposure without linking to well-testing or water-source data.

## Evidence Quality Rule

Evidence is not accepted because it sounds plausible. It is accepted when the source, method, limitations, and confidence are explicit enough for a reviewer to attack.
