# Arsenic-Contaminated Groundwater Exposure And Testing Coverage Gaps In Bangladesh

## Problem Statement

Bangladesh has the largest documented population exposed to arsenic-contaminated groundwater in the world. Tube wells installed since the 1970s for bacterial-disease prevention inadvertently tapped arsenic-rich aquifers. The result is a chronic exposure crisis affecting an estimated 35-77 million people above the WHO guideline of 10 micro-grams per litre.

The v0 problem is not "is arsenic harmful?" That is settled science. The sharper problem is:

> Which upazilas have the largest gap between known arsenic contamination and household-level testing coverage, and where would additional testing resources most reduce exposure?

## Known Facts

- Verified fact: WHO classifies arsenic as one of the 10 chemicals of major public health concern and sets the drinking-water guideline at 10 micro-grams per litre (WHO, 2022).
- Verified fact: The British Geological Survey and DPHE surveyed 3,534 tube wells across Bangladesh in 1998-1999 and found that 27 percent exceeded 50 micro-grams per litre (the Bangladesh standard at the time) and 46 percent exceeded 10 micro-grams per litre (the WHO guideline).
- Verified fact: A 2024 systematic review in Environmental Health Perspectives estimated that 35-77 million people in Bangladesh have been exposed to arsenic above the WHO guideline, with exposure varying sharply by upazila and depth.
- Verified fact: The Bangladesh Arsenic Mitigation Water Supply Project (BAMWSP) tested over 1.4 million tube wells by 2006, but coverage is uneven and millions of wells remain untested, particularly in remote and newly-installed wells.

## Uncertain Areas

- The current testing coverage rate at upazila or union grain is not systematically reported in a single public dataset.
- The relationship between well depth and arsenic concentration varies by geology and is not uniform across Bangladesh.
- Whether testing-coverage gap maps change actual resource allocation by the national arsenic mitigation programme.
- How household-level behavioural responses to testing (switching wells, treating water) interact with testing-coverage gaps.

## Initial Scope

This pack accepts work on:

- Evidence inventory of arsenic survey, testing, and health-outcome data sources.
- Dataset availability and licensing for sub-national analysis.
- Upazila-level testing-coverage gap analysis.
- Reproducible exposure-risk mapping from public survey data.
- Red-team review of harmful operational interpretations (stigmatising affected regions, discouraging well use without alternatives).
- Field-reality review from Bangladesh public-health or local implementation contexts.

This pack does not accept direct medical advice, well-closure orders, or water-supply infrastructure recommendations.

## Done Condition For V0

The pack is useful when it can produce one reviewed and replicated upazila-level testing-coverage gap analysis, with clear limits, that a domain reviewer judges suitable for informing national arsenic mitigation programme resource allocation discussion.
