# Occupational Heat Stress And Worker Health In Outdoor Industries Under Climate Change

## Problem Statement

Climate change is increasing wet-bulb globe temperature (WBGT) across the tropics and subtropics. Outdoor workers in agriculture, construction, mining, and transport face the highest exposure because their work is physically demanding and occurs during peak heat hours. The ILO estimates that 2.2 billion workers are exposed to excessive heat and that heat-related occupational injuries cause 23 million DALYs annually. Yet in most LMICs, occupational heat stress is not a reportable condition, labor inspectorates have no exposure data, and workplace heat standards are either absent or unenforced.

The v0 problem is not "stop climate change" or "protect all workers." Those framings are too broad for a single pack. The sharper problem is:

> Which outdoor worker populations in low- and middle-income countries face the highest evidence-backed heat exposure burden, and would a verified exposure-outcome map change where labor ministries and the ILO direct worker protection resources?

## Known Facts

- Verified fact: ILO estimates 2.2 billion workers are exposed to excessive heat globally, with work-related injuries from heat stress accounting for approximately 23 million DALYs annually (ILO, 2024).
- Verified fact: The proportion of workers exposed to excessive heat is highest in LMICs: the ILO reports that 76 percent of the working population in low-income countries is exposed, compared to 42 percent in high-income countries (ILO, 2024).
- Verified fact: A 2021 systematic review and meta-analysis in Environment International found that extreme heat is associated with increased occupational injuries with a pooled odds ratio of 1.21 (95 percent CI: 1.11-1.32), with substantial heterogeneity across climate zones and industries (Xu et al., 2021).
- Verified fact: The WHO/ILO joint estimation report (2024) calculated that non-heat-related occupational exposure to solar ultraviolet radiation causes approximately 19,000 deaths per year from non-melanoma skin cancer, concentrated in outdoor workers, demonstrating that occupational exposure quantification for climate-linked hazards is methodologically feasible.
- Verified fact: ISO 7243 provides an international standard for WBGT measurement and heat stress assessment, defining thresholds for work-rest regimes by metabolic rate and clothing. This standard exists but is rarely implemented in LMIC occupational health systems.
- Verified fact: A 2018 study in Nature Climate Change (Dunne et al.) projected that heat stress will reduce global labor capacity in vulnerable regions by up to 20 percent by 2050 under RCP8.5, with the largest losses in South Asia, West Africa, and Southeast Asia.

## Uncertain Areas

- Sub-national geographic distribution of outdoor worker populations by industry within LMICs. Labor force surveys report industry at national level but rarely at sub-national grain, and informal sector workers are typically excluded.
- Actual health outcomes from occupational heat exposure in LMICs. Most evidence on heat-related occupational injury comes from high-income countries (US, Australia, Spain); extrapolation to LMIC contexts with different work practices, acclimatization, and health system access is uncertain.
- Whether WBGT thresholds derived from temperate-climate studies apply to chronically heat-exposed tropical populations. Some evidence suggests acclimatization shifts the dose-response curve, but the magnitude is debated.
- The contribution of informal sector work to total occupational heat exposure burden. In many LMICs, 60-80 percent of the workforce is informal and invisible to occupational health surveillance.
- Whether heat-related productivity losses translate to income losses for workers or to output losses for employers. This distinction matters for who bears the cost and who should act.

## Initial Scope

This pack accepts work on:

- Evidence inventory of WBGT data sources, labor force distribution data, occupational health surveillance data, and heat-health epidemiology studies.
- Dataset compilation of WBGT exposure estimates linked to outdoor worker population estimates for selected LMICs.
- Exposure-outcome analysis for one to three LMICs where both exposure and outcome data exist at sufficient grain.
- Worker protection intervention evidence review (rest breaks, hydration, scheduling shifts, PPE, acclimatization protocols) with failure modes and field constraints.
- Red-team review of how exposure maps could be misused to justify employer negligence claims, shift blame to workers, or rationalize exclusion from employment.
- Field-reality review from labor ministries, labor inspectorates, employer associations, or worker organizations.

This pack does not accept clinical treatment advice, employer liability determinations, or workplace safety enforcement directives.

## Done Condition For V0

The pack is useful when it can produce one reviewed and replicated sub-national exposure-outcome artifact for at least one LMIC, with clear limits on exposure metric, worker population coverage, and outcome data quality, that a domain reviewer judges suitable for worker protection resource allocation discussion rather than immediate enforcement.
