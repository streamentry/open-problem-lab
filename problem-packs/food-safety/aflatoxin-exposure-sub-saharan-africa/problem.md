# Aflatoxin Exposure From Contaminated Staple Grains In Sub-Saharan Africa

## Problem Statement

Sub-Saharan Africa consumes maize and groundnuts as daily staples for hundreds of millions of people. Both crops are susceptible to Aspergillus flavus infection, which produces aflatoxin B1, an IARC Group 1 carcinogen. Chronic dietary exposure drives hepatocellular carcinoma, contributes to child stunting, and suppresses immune function. Acute aflatoxicosis outbreaks have killed dozens in Kenya and Tanzania. Yet no sub-national exposure map exists for any SSA country, and post-harvest intervention resources are allocated without geographic targeting.

The v0 problem is not "eliminate aflatoxin." That framing is too broad for a single pack. The sharper problem is:

> Which districts in sub-Saharan Africa carry the highest evidence-backed aflatoxin contamination burden in staple grains, and would a verified risk map change where post-harvest drying, biocontrol, and surveillance testing are deployed?

## Known Facts

- Verified fact: IARC classifies aflatoxin B1 as a Group 1 human carcinogen, with sufficient evidence for liver cancer causation, especially in HBV-positive individuals.
- Verified fact: WHO estimates that aflatoxin exposure contributes to a measurable fraction of the 25,000-155,000 annual global liver cancer deaths, with the highest exposure-attributable fractions in Africa and Asia.
- Verified fact: EPA aflatoxin reference dose is 0.3 ng/kg body weight per day for non-cancer effects; Codex Alimentarius sets maximum limits of 10 micrograms per kg for ready-to-eat peanuts and 5 micrograms per kg for milk.
- Verified fact: A 2010 WHO report estimated that 4.5 to 5.5 billion people in developing countries are chronically exposed to aflatoxin, with the highest per-capita exposure in sub-Saharan Africa.
- Verified fact: Aflasafe, a biocontrol product using non-toxigenic Aspergillus strains, has demonstrated efficacy in field trials across Nigeria, Kenya, and Senegal, reducing aflatoxin contamination by 60-90 percent.
- Verified fact: Acute aflatoxicosis outbreaks in Kenya (2004, 2005, 2006) caused 317 cases with 125 deaths from contaminated maize, with contamination levels reaching 4,400 micrograms per kg, far exceeding Codex limits.

## Uncertain Areas

- Sub-national geographic distribution of contamination hotspots within SSA countries. Most published surveys cover convenience samples from one or two markets, not nationally representative sampling frames.
- How climate change will shift the geographic range of Aspergillus suitability, particularly the interaction between drought stress, insect damage, and pre-harvest infection.
- Whether biocontrol deployment at scale can be sustained in smallholder farming systems without continued extension support.
- The relative contribution of pre-harvest versus post-harvest contamination to total dietary exposure, which determines whether interventions should target field biocontrol, drying practices, or storage technologies.
- The true disease burden: liver cancer is the most quantified outcome, but aflatoxin's contribution to stunting and immunosuppression remains debated in causal strength and magnitude.

## Initial Scope

This pack accepts work on:

- Evidence inventory of mycotoxin test data, climate suitability models, and post-harvest practice surveys.
- Dataset compilation of contamination test results with geographic grain, crop type, test method, and sampling frame.
- District-level contamination risk mapping for one to three SSA countries where data density permits.
- Biocontrol intervention evidence review with failure modes and field-reality constraints.
- Red-team review of how risk maps could be misused to stigmatize regions, restrict trade, or justify punitive inspection.
- Field-reality review from food safety authorities, extension services, or public health implementers.

This pack does not accept clinical treatment advice, trade restriction recommendations, or declarations of food safety emergencies.

## Done Condition For V0

The pack is useful when it can produce one reviewed and replicated district-level contamination risk artifact for at least one SSA country, with clear limits on sampling frame, test method, and geographic extrapolation, that a domain reviewer judges suitable for post-harvest intervention allocation discussion rather than immediate deployment.
