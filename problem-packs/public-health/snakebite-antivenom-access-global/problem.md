# Snakebite Envenoming Mortality And Antivenom Access Gaps In Rural Low-Income Countries

## Problem Statement

WHO estimates 1.8 to 2.7 million snakebite envenomings per year, causing 81,410 to 137,880 deaths and approximately three times as many amputations and permanent disabilities. Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia carry 85 percent of the burden. Antivenom is the only effective treatment, yet stock-outs are common in high-burden districts and counterfeit or inappropriate antivenoms are widespread. Under-reporting is severe: a community-level study in India estimated 45,900 deaths in 2005, over 30 times higher than government-reported figures. Mapping snakebite incidence against facility-level antivenom stocking at district scale is the missing operational tool.

> Which sub-national areas in high-burden LMICs have the highest evidence-backed gap between estimated snakebite mortality risk and documented antivenom access?

## Known Facts

- Verified fact: WHO estimates 1.8 to 2.7 million envenomings per year, with 81,410 to 137,880 deaths and approximately three times as many amputations annually. SSA and South Asia carry 85 percent of the burden.
- Verified fact: WHO added snakebite envenoming to the neglected tropical disease list in 2017 and launched a strategy targeting 50 percent mortality and disability reduction by 2030.
- Verified fact: Under-reporting is severe. In India, a community-level study estimated 45,900 deaths in 2005, over 30 times higher than government-reported figures. Revised estimates suggest 1.2 million Indians died from snakebite envenoming between 2000 and 2019 (average 58,000 per year).
- Verified fact: Antivenom production has declined, with several manufacturers ceasing production and prices increasing dramatically, creating supply crises particularly in sub-Saharan Africa.
- Verified fact: Agricultural population maps and snakebite incidence surveys exist but have not been systematically linked to facility-level antivenom stocking data.

## Uncertain Areas

- Whether snakebite incidence can be modeled from agricultural population and snake-habitat proxies where community surveys are absent.
- Whether antivenom supply-chain and facility-stocking data is accessible at sub-national scale in any high-burden country.
- Whether modeled burden estimates are precise enough at country level to drive antivenom procurement prioritization.
- Whether antivenom access-gap maps change procurement or distribution decisions, or are used to justify centralized stockpiling without addressing last-mile distribution.

## Initial Scope

This pack accepts work on:

- Evidence inventory and source quality assessment.
- Dataset availability including WHO burden data, antivenom production data, facility-stocking surveys, agricultural population maps, and snake habitat models.
- Sub-national snakebite risk mapping combining incidence, agricultural population, and habitat proxies.
- Reproducible antivenom access-gap analysis comparing risk to facility-level stocking and travel-time.
- Red-team review of misuse including stigmatization of traditional healers, procurement centralization without last-mile distribution, and misattribution of modeled estimates as measured incidence.
- Field-reality review with snakebite program planning context.

This pack does not accept clinical treatment guidelines, antivenom procurement mandates, or unvalidated risk scores presented as definitive access measurements.

## Done Condition For V0

The pack is useful when it can produce one reviewed and replicated sub-national antivenom access-gap analysis for at least two high-burden countries, with explicit validation limitations, that a domain reviewer judges suitable for further discussion rather than immediate procurement action.
