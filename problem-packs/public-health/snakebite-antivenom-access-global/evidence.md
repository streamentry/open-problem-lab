## Evidence Notes

### WHO Snakebite Envenoming Strategy (2019)

The foundational WHO strategy document. Provides global burden estimates (1.8-2.7 million envenomings, 81,410-137,880 deaths annually) and the 2030 target of 50 percent mortality and disability reduction. Use this as the reference burden estimate and strategy framing. Limitation: burden estimates are modeled from surveys in a limited number of high-burden countries and extrapolated globally.

### WHO Snakebite Fact Sheet (2023)

Updated WHO fact sheet with revised burden ranges and under-reporting evidence. The India comparison (45,900 community-estimated deaths vs government-reported figures, a 30x gap) is the strongest single data point on surveillance inadequacy. Use this for framing and under-reporting context. Limitation: global summary, no sub-national data.

### Gutiérrez et al. 2017 (Nature Reviews Disease Primers)

The most comprehensive peer-reviewed scientific overview of snakebite envenoming. Covers pathophysiology, epidemiology, diagnosis, antivenom production, and treatment access. Documents the antivenom production decline and supply crisis. Use this as the scientific reference for the disease and treatment landscape. Limitation: published in 2017; the production landscape may have shifted.

### Gutiérrez et al. 2014 (WHO Bulletin)

Documents the antivenom supply crisis: manufacturers ceasing production, prices rising, stock-outs in high-burden countries. Proposes a multicomponent strategy combining improved epidemiological data, regulatory quality control, and strategic stockpiling. Use this for understanding structural supply barriers. Limitation: published in 2014; the supply situation has evolved.

### Lao PDR Antivenom Cost-Effectiveness (2026)

A cost-effectiveness study demonstrating that antivenom access interventions can be evaluated at sub-national level with urban-rural distributional analysis. Rural populations bear the highest burden from delayed access. Use this as a methodology reference for antivenom access evaluation. Limitation: single-country study; methodology may not transfer directly.

### Africa Antivenom Policy Analysis (2026)

Identifies key barriers to equitable antivenom access in Africa: fragmented supply chains, weak regulatory frameworks, insufficient national procurement, and lack of sub-national burden data. Proposes regional manufacturing coordination and demand forecasting. Use this for policy context and barrier identification. Limitation: policy analysis, not primary data; Africa-focused.

## Evidence Quality Rule

Evidence is not accepted because it sounds plausible. It is accepted when the source, method, limitations, and confidence are explicit enough for a reviewer to attack.
