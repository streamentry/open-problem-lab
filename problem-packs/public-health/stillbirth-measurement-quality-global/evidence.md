## Evidence Notes

### 2021 Lancet systematic assessment on stillbirth trends

Use this source for global and regional burden framing. It is the right anchor for scale and geographic concentration. It is not enough for sub-national prioritization or for distinguishing quality failure from counting failure.

### 2016 Lancet paper on ending preventable stillbirths by 2030

Use this source for the policy wedge around preventability and the special relevance of intrapartum stillbirth. Do not use it as proof that any current country has the same intervention readiness or causal mix today.

### 2016 Lancet paper on stillbirth rates and risk factors

Use this source for the argument that burden concentration and acceleration differ by region and health-system capacity. It supports pack selection. It does not solve present-day measurement comparability.

### DHS Guide to Statistics: perinatal mortality

The DHS guide is usable for a reproducible survey-measure definition. It states that the stillbirth rate uses fetal deaths in pregnancies of at least 28 weeks or seven months divided by stillbirths plus live births, and that DHS-8 derives stillbirths from pregnancy histories while earlier surveys used the contraceptive calendar. It is useful for measure-family reconciliation, but not for treating survey recall as a complete vital-registration count or for inferring intrapartum timing.

### DHS-8 interviewer manual

The interviewer manual documents the pregnancy-outcome question, pregnancy duration, and confirmation that a reported stillbirth was born without crying, moving, or breathing. It is useful for checking instrument wording and classification mechanics. It cannot establish reporting completeness, clinical gestational-age accuracy, or facility-level quality.

### WHO institutional stillbirth indicator metadata

WHO's indicator metadata specifies facility stillbirth measurement, including antepartum versus intrapartum timing, gestational age, birthweight, facility denominator, and the recommended 28-week threshold for international reporting. It is the strongest source here for defining what a facility indicator must contain. It does not prove that a country collects those fields completely or that a facility rate is causal evidence of poor care.

### UNICEF stillbirth definition and HMIS data-quality assessment

The UNICEF assessment is usable as a data-quality audit framework for health-management information systems. It identifies vital status at birth, gestational age, and birthweight as core classification fields and links data quality to program use. It cannot produce a comparable rate for a country without the underlying record-level data, and it does not remove referral or case-mix bias from facility observations.

### WHO/UNICEF MPDSR materials

The 2021 MPDSR guidance supports a facility and community workflow for identifying, notifying, reviewing, and responding to maternal and perinatal deaths, including stillbirths. Its explicit blame-free and confidentiality framing is relevant to interpreting facility records without creating reporting disincentives. It is implementation guidance, not evidence that any named country has complete case ascertainment or that a reviewed death was preventable.

### WHO CRVS quality and use assessment

The WHO CRVS assessment toolkit is usable for system-level classification of registration completeness, legal framework, information flows, and data-quality checks. It can tell a reviewer whether CRVS counts are ready for further analysis, but it is not itself a stillbirth prevalence dataset and cannot substitute for a country-specific completeness assessment.

## Evidence Quality Rule

Stillbirth evidence is unusually vulnerable to definition drift, silence in administrative systems, and false comparability across source families. An evidence record is only useful here if it helps a reviewer say what the source cannot prove.
