# Substandard And Falsified Medicine Detection And Surveillance Gaps In Low- And Middle-Income Countries

## Problem Statement

Poor-quality medicines are not a fringe problem. WHO estimates that approximately 1 in 10 medical products in low- and middle-income countries is substandard or falsified. The Lancet Global Health Burden of Antimicrobial Resistance study estimated that poor-quality antimalarials alone contribute to approximately 200,000-450,000 additional child deaths annually in sub-Saharan Africa. Substandard antibiotics are a direct driver of antimicrobial resistance. Falsified medicines erode trust in health systems and kill through both treatment failure and direct toxicity.

But the problem is harder than the headline. "Substandard" and "falsified" are not the same failure mode. Substandard medicines fail quality specifications due to poor manufacturing, degradation, or improper storage — they may originate from licensed producers. Falsified medicines are deliberately and fraudulently mislabeled with respect to identity or source. These require different surveillance strategies: substandard products demand post-market quality testing and supply-chain storage audits; falsified products demand track-and-trace systems, customs interdiction, and criminal investigation. Conflating them — as most aggregate estimates do — produces surveillance recommendations that miss both problems.

The second structural problem is that post-market quality surveillance in most LMICs is geographically sparse, chronically underfunded, and concentrated in capital-city laboratories. A national medicines regulatory authority cannot answer the question a district pharmacist or a global procurer actually needs answered: which products in which supply-chain tiers in which districts are failing quality tests, and where is no testing being done at all?

> Which sub-national areas and product categories in LMICs have the highest evidence-backed gap between medicine-quality failure risk and post-market surveillance coverage, such that a procurement officer or regulator could redirect quality-control resources?

## Known Facts

- Verified fact: WHO's 2017 global report estimates that approximately 1 in 10 medical products in low- and middle-income countries is substandard or falsified, with higher rates in sub-Saharan Africa and Southeast Asia.
- Verified fact: A 2019 Lancet Global Health modeling study estimated that poor-quality antimalarials contribute to approximately 200,000-450,000 additional deaths annually among children under five in sub-Saharan Africa.
- Verified fact: WHO's Global Surveillance and Monitoring System (GSMS) received reports of over 1,500 substandard or falsified medical products between 2013 and 2017, but coverage is limited to participating countries and relies on passive reporting.
- Verified fact: Substandard antibiotics — products that contain insufficient active pharmaceutical ingredient — are a direct driver of antimicrobial resistance, as sub-therapeutic concentrations expose pathogens to selective pressure without killing them.
- Verified fact: A 2022 systematic review of 51 field surveys in LMICs found failure rates ranging from 12% to 44% for antimalarials and 11% to 48% for antibiotics, with wide heterogeneity by product class, supply-chain tier, and country.

## Uncertain Areas

- Whether field-survey failure rates generalize to national populations given sampling bias toward high-suspicion products and convenience sampling at pharmacy level.
- Whether post-market surveillance density (laboratories per district, testing frequency) is measurable enough to produce a coverage-gap map.
- Whether market-distribution data from import records, pharmacy chains, and informal-sector surveys can be linked to quality-testing results at product-lot granularity.
- Whether a quality-gap map would change procurement decisions or regulator inspection schedules without triggering panic-driven market withdrawals that reduce overall medicine access.
- How to distinguish substandard from falsified products in aggregate surveillance data when most LMIC testing protocols do not include forensic packaging analysis.

## Initial Scope

This pack accepts work on:

- Evidence inventory separating WHO GSMS data, national quality surveillance reports, peer-reviewed field-survey studies, and supply-chain distribution data into usable, limited, and rejected categories.
- Measurement notes that reconcile the difference between substandard (quality failure) and falsified (fraudulent mislabeling) failure modes and prohibit conflated reporting.
- Country selection memos that identify where sub-national quality-surveillance work is genuinely possible rather than performatively global.
- Surveillance-gap analysis asking where post-market testing density is too low to detect quality failures relative to market volume and known risk factors.
- Red-team review of panic-driven withdrawal, market-distortion, and supply-chain-gaming risks.
- Field-reality review with national medicines regulatory authorities, procurement officers, and supply-chain managers.

This pack does not accept blanket medicine-import bans, brand-specific procurement recommendations without replicated testing evidence, individual patient treatment advice, or unvalidated quality scores presented as definitive product assessments.

## Done Condition For V0

The pack is useful when it can produce one reviewed and replicated sub-national surveillance-gap note for at least one high-burden country that:

- separates substandard from falsified failure modes in its methodology
- documents which product categories and supply-chain tiers have been tested and which have not
- names the procurement or regulatory decision the gap map could change
- identifies where the evidence is too weak for operational inference
