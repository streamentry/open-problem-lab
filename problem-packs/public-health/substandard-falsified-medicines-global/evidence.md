## Evidence Notes

### WHO Fact Sheet on SF Medical Products

Use this source for the global prevalence estimate (1 in 10 medical products in LMICs) and for WHO's definitions distinguishing substandard from falsified products. Do not use the aggregate estimate as a country-level or product-level failure rate. The fact sheet combines two distinct failure modes under one prevalence figure.

### Renschler et al. 2015

Use this source for the modeled mortality estimate of poor-quality antimalarials in sub-Saharan Africa (approximately 122,000-158,000 additional under-five deaths per year). Treat this as a modeled range, not a direct count. The study combines falsified, substandard, and degraded products into one category, which limits failure-mode-specific conclusions. Quality-survey inputs may be biased toward high-suspicion products.

### WHO GSMS

Use this source for understanding the structure and limitations of the global surveillance system. GSMS is passive-reporting and participation-limited; absence of reports from a country does not indicate absence of poor-quality products. Do not use GSMS report counts as a denominator for national failure rates.

### Cavany et al. 2023 (Nature Communications)

Use this source for the mechanistic link between sub-therapeutic antimicrobial concentrations and resistance selection, and critically, for the authors' explicit conclusion that the population-level evidence for SF medicines as an AMR driver is uncertain. The AMR-driver claim must carry this uncertainty caveat. Do not attribute a specific share of total AMR burden to SF medicines alone.

## Evidence Quality Rule

Evidence is not accepted because it sounds plausible. It is accepted when the source, method, limitations, and confidence are explicit enough for a reviewer to attack. For medicine-quality claims, the reviewer must be able to check the product category, test method, geographic grain, and sampling frame.
